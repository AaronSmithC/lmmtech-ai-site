A Warning

We Must Give Our Children a Better Offer

Aaron Smith  ·  ~5 min read

We have optimized the instrument and neglected the goal. The economy is a tool for producing good lives — stability, family, community, room to aspire — yet we increasingly manage it as though the scoreboard were the point. Rising markets and rising credentials are counted as success even as the lives underneath them stall. That is a category error, and the generation coming of age is paying its cost.

The evidence is not subtle. A record 25.2 million adults under 35 lived with their parents in 2025 — roughly one in three. The reflex is to read this as failure to launch, but the numbers refuse that story: about 70 percent of those aged 25 to 34 living at home hold jobs, a share essentially unchanged in a quarter century. These are not idle young people. They are working and still cannot afford the first step into an independent life. The median home listing price reached $430,000 last year, up 34 percent since 2019; median rent climbed to roughly $1,673. Education, once a reliable ladder, now often delivers debt without the security it used to promise. We are teaching the next generation, through outcomes more than words, that the deal is no longer competitive. Fail to deliver a better offer and we court a high-tech dystopia of eroded trust, wasted potential, and quiet disengagement.

Look past the polished indicators. Official statistics celebrate rising attainment and occasional gains for young households, but averages hide distribution: the poorest in the cohort often fare worse than prior generations at the same age, while housing scarcity and debt blunt progress in the middle. Headline employment figures miss the long-term disconnected — discouraged workers, those sidelined by disability or skills mismatch, people cycling through precarious arrangements. These "missing" are real human experience of exclusion, not statistical noise. When capable people sense diminishing returns, they adjust rationally: they delay independence, family formation, and full commitment. The result never shows up cleanly in GDP, but it is felt in postponed lives and lowered expectations.

The mechanism deserves scrutiny, because it explains why growth and hardship coexist. New money enters the economy through banks and asset markets first, lifting equities and real estate — stores of value — before it reaches the people who need shelter and breathing room. So the scoreboard climbs while the ground floor does not. The stimulus experiments of the early 2020s briefly bypassed that spigot and delivered tangible results: sharp drops in child poverty, fewer evictions, small businesses surviving because customers could still spend. Those outcomes mattered more than the old scoreboard admitted. Then we reverted, leaving the structural bias intact.

A better offer starts from the other end: build the economy backward from the lives it is meant to enable. Universal direct mechanisms — flat, available to all, not need-tested — deserve serious consideration, for practical as much as moral reasons. Means-testing draws subjective lines that get lobbied, gamed, and captured by bureaucracy, and it sorts people into "deserving" and "undeserving" — a distinction that corrodes both politics and dignity. Universal removes the line. Everyone receives, just as everyone pays taxes; those who don't need the check can give it away or let progressive taxation reclaim it. Receiving stops being an admission of failure and becomes a floor — one that frees people to take risks, leave bad jobs, form households earlier, or invest in themselves and their children.

The strongest objection is inflation, and it deserves a direct answer rather than a dismissal. A universal transfer that is not matched by real output can raise prices — but that risk is a function of magnitude, funding, and what the money is spent on, not an automatic law. Fund it substantially through progressive taxation rather than pure issuance and the net new demand shrinks. Pair it with expanded supply in exactly the sectors young people are priced out of, and the transfer chases more goods rather than a fixed stock. The failure mode is a floor bolted onto an economy still rigged for scarcity; the design that works couples the floor with abundance. Concerns about labor supply and fiscal cost are legitimate too, and the honest response is that pilots and stimulus rounds show direct household support improving material conditions, child outcomes, and agency without the administrative drag of targeted programs — evidence to build on, not proof of a free lunch.

The politics are shifting under all of this. The current arrangement quietly favors those already holding assets and credentials — the people scarcity prices in rather than out — and that group is shrinking as a share of the population, while the generation facing the raw deal is growing in weight. A tipping point is approaching. Pressure is building, and change is coming — messy or constructive, depending on how we respond.

Delivering a better offer does not mean abandoning growth or incentives. It means pairing the universal floor with radical abundance in the basics: housing supply through zoning reform and construction, skills pathways with real returns beyond expensive degrees, and policy that favors broad-based productivity in energy, technology, and entrepreneurship. A floor plus supply-side abundance raises real living standards instead of merely subsidizing high costs. Work stays central — meaning, purpose, and contribution matter — but survival need not be fully hostage to formal employment in a world of technological abundance.

The warning is straightforward. Give the next generation reasons to invest fully in this society. Restore the believable path to independence, family, and security. Prioritize the good life over optimizing the instrument, and let the numbers we obsess over serve human flourishing rather than obscure it. We have the tools — technological progress, experimental evidence, and mounting pressure from below — to do better. The question is whether we use them before the tipping point turns constructive pressure into something harder. Our children are watching, and their lived experience will judge us.
